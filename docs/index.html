How this works

The short version: we estimate how good each team is from its runs scored and runs allowed (which predict the future better than win-loss record does), blend in the actual record, and stay skeptical of small sample sizes. This is why a .500 team with a big positive run differential can post playoff odds that look "too high" next to its record: the model is betting the runs are telling the truth and the record will catch up. Then we play out every remaining game on the schedule thousands of times on a virtual coin weighted by those strengths, plus a home-field bump, and count how often each team finishes top-4 in points percentage and how often it wins the whole thing.

The league's quirks are built in: standings use points percentage (2 points per win, 1 point for losing a game decided by the Home Run Derby tiebreaker), ties break by total points and then head-to-head, and the playoffs are best-of-3 semifinals (1v4, 2v3) and a best-of-3 final where the higher seed hosts games 1 and 3.

Summer ball is chaos, and the model knows it: rosters turn over mid-season: MLB Draft departures, school commitments, innings limits. In 2025, Nashua fell from playoff position to 24–36 while a sub-.500 Norwich took the 4-seed and won its third straight title. So in every simulated season, each team's strength gets a random mid-summer swing (the "roster churn" dial, default ±12%). Most seasons it's small; sometimes it's a full-on collapse or a hot-roster surge. That's why nobody here gets a 99.9% and nobody gets a 0.1% while there's still a month of baseball left.

Recent form counts: team strength leans partly on each club's last 12 games (30% by default, adjustable), because in a summer league the roster playing tonight is not always the roster that played in June. When a team genuinely changes mid-season, the model starts to see it within a couple of weeks.

What the model still can't see: which roster will be the one that changes next. Churn is random in the simulations; you may actually know things it doesn't. That's what the per-team strength dials are for. If you know a team just lost its ace, turn them down and watch.

Honesty note: these are estimates, not promises. A 90% chance loses one time in ten. Early-season odds move a lot; that's the math working, not breaking.
